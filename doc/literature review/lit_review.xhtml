1 Introduction

Ecological sustainability and the sustainability of human development is rapidly becoming a greater concern for the global and local populations and the policy makers tasked with governance (Lubchenco, 1998).

2 Climate change

There is a large body of evidence that we are living in the beginning of an era of massive anthropogenic climate change. The increasing human population coupled with the increasing intensity of human consumption and economic activity is placing unprecedented strain on the environment (Vitousek et al., 1997). The effects of anthropogenic climate change are wide reaching, affecting all aspects of the environment and human society(Lubchenco, 1998).
The global population is becoming increasingly vulnerable to effects of both environmental disaster and slow environmental degradation (Lubchenco, 1998). Not only is there a risk of massive loss of human life, but the increasing pressures on resources and land availability make even small environmental changes capable of displacing local populations and threatening food security, leading to economic and political instability. These risks are especially pertinent to Sub-Saharan Africa as well as much of the rest of the developing world(Brown and Funk, 2008; Bloem et al., 2010).
The International Panel on Climate Change (IPCC), a committee convened by the United Nations to investigate climate change, is unequivocal that a large component of the increases in global temperatures are caused anthropogenic green house gases (IPCC, 2007). Its further states that even if green house gas emissions were to be stabilized global warming and rising sea levels would continue for centuries, stating quite simply:
“Anthropogenic warming and sea level rise would continue for centuries due to the time scales associated with climate processes and feedbacks, even if GHG (Green house gas) concentrations were to be stabilised. {WGI 10.4, 10.5, 10.7, SPM}” (IPCC, 2007)
Predictions about climate change are heavily contingent on the assumptions made about future human activity. Because of this the IPCC has devloped a number of possible scenarios in the Special Report on Emissions Scenarios (IPCC, 2000) which were then updated in (IPCC, 2007). Forty scenarios were developed and grouped in to 4 scenario families, that are representative the range of predicted possible futures (IPCC, 2000). These scenario families, listed in a rough order of severity, are B1, B2, A1, A2 and are divided based on the global integration of nations and the focus of policy.
